Evacuation Procedures

Back

The following are some helpful tips in the event of a facilities evacuation:

EVACUATION PLAN

  • The plan provides guidance and procedures on how to appropriately and effectively evacuate, should the need arise as a result of an internal or external emergency or disaster.
  • Learning the plan in advance ensures prompt response, appropriate care of patients, and safety of our visitors and staff in the event that evacuating our facilities becomes necessary.

AUTHORITY TO EVACUATE

  • Any employee has the authority to implement a LOCAL evacuation of patients or visitors from an area in which an immediate life-threatening situation exists in order to avoid or minimize injuries.
  • The hospitals' CEO (or their designee) and/or Los Angeles City Fire or Police Departments have the authority to order a TOTAL evacuation.

TYPES OF EVACUATIONS

The plan describes two types of evacuation:

  • Partial: Where a given area is uninhabitable for patient safety, requiring in-house transfer, transfer to another facility or discharge.
  • Total: Disruption or discontinuance of services caused by a power outage, natural disaster or other calamity that damages the facilities and threatens the safety and welfare of patients, visitors and staff.

EVACUATION EQUIPMENT

  • Evacuation equipment includes: wheelchairs, gurneys, Med-Sleds, backboards, sheets, blankets, etc.
  • All available equipment that can be used to evacuate non-ambulatory patients will be brought to a centralized location (to be determined at the time of the event) so that a coordinated and organized evacuation can occur.

EVACUATION METHODS

  • Local: movement of patients from one room to another room in the same unit.
  • Horizontal: movement of patients to a safe area on the same floor.
  • Vertical: movement of patients towards the ground floor using stairwells.

WHAT SHOULD I DO?

  • Read the entire Hospital Evacuation plan, EM 1-106, on the intranet.
  • Ensure patient safety and comfort at all times.
  • If an evacuation is ordered, evacuate away from danger.
  • Do not use elevators unless specifically informed that it is safe to do so.
  • If an evacuation is ordered, please observe staff and colleagues for physical injuries and/or symptoms of shock.

BOMB THREAT CALL PROCEDURES

Bomb Threats are serious until proven otherwise.

Act quickly, but remain calm. Obtain information with the checklist on the reverse of this document.

IF A BOMB THREAT IS RECIEVED BY PHONE

  • Remain calm. Keep the caller on the line for as long as possible. DO NOT HANG UP, Even if the caller does.
  • Listen carefully. Be polite and show interest
  • Try to keep the caller talking to learn more information
  • If phone has a display, copy the number and/or letters on the window display
  • Complete the Bomb Threat Checklist (reverse side) immediately. Write down as much detail as you can remember. Try to get extact words.
  • Immediately upon termination of the call, do not hang up, but from a different phone make contact with your supervisor as outlines below

IF YOU RECIEVE A WRITTEN THREAT

  • Gather all materials as evidence, including any envelopes or containers.
  • Avoid further handling to prevent the contamination of evidence
  • Notify your supervisor.
  • Follow contact procedures below

SIGNS OF A SUSPICIOUS PACKAGE

  • No return address
  • Excessive Postage
  • Stains
  • Strange odors
  • Strange sounds
  • Unexpected delivery
  • Poorly handwritten
  • Misspelled words
  • Incorrect titles
  • Foreign
  • Restrictive notes

UNATTENDED ITEMS IF A SUSPICIOUS LETTER OR PACKAGE

If you have a suspicious package as described above,and unable to verify the contents.

  • Handle the item with care. Do not shake or bump
  • Do not open, smell or taste the article
  • Isolate the mailing and secure the immediate area
  • Do not put in water or in a confined space, such as a desk drawer or cabinet
  • If possible, open windows in the immediate area to assit in venting possible eplosove gasses.
  • Follow contact procedures below

DO NOT:

  • Use two-way radios or cellular phone;radio signals have potential to detonate a bomb
  • Evacuate the building until police arrive and evaluate the threat
  • Activate the Fire Alram
  • Touch or move a suspicious package

WHO TO CONTACT

KECK and NORRIS Hospitals DIAL 77, the operator will notify the AOD Immediately

HCC I and HSC II DIAL 21000. Then dial 323-442-8500 and notify the hospital operator and request that they notify the AOD immediately

ALL OTHER LOCATIONS Dial 9, then 911. THen dial 323-442-8500 and notify the hospital operator and request that they notify the AOD immediately.

Earthquake Response

ABOUT EARTHQUAKES

Earthquakes strike without warning and, in most cases, the shock begins and ends within seconds. The movement of the ground in an earthquake is seldom the direct cause of death or injury. Most casualties result from falling objects and debris. That is why it’s important to ensure that your work area is free from unnecessary overhead objects.

Here are some more safety tips:

ALWAYS RESPOND IMMEDIATELY

  • Upon detection of movement, DUCK, COVER AND HOLD ON. Remain in place until the shaking stops. Be alert for aftershocks.
  • Stay away from windows and mirrors. Get under a table, desk, bed, or in a corner away from windows. Encourage others to follow your example. Do not run outdoors.
  • After the initial shock has ended, and a reasonable interval has passed with no further aftershocks, survey immediate surroundings to determine injuries and damage.

REMAIN CALM

  • Remain calm, and try to calm and aware of your surroundings.
  • If possible, reassure and attempt to calm those who may be hysterical or panic-stricken.

REMAIN CALM

  • If indoors, watch for falling plaster, light fixtures, ceiling tiles and other objects.
  • Watch out for high storage areas, shelves and tall equipment that might slide or topple.

TAKE CAREFUL ACTION

  • If there are obvious injuries from falling objects, shattered glass or if patients or personnel are trapped under debris, you must request assistance and perform first aid within your capability where possible until additional medical personnel arrive to assist.
  • Do not attempt to lead or assist any patients out of the facility until you are directed to do so by evacuation order. (See Hospital Evacuation Plan)

HOW CAN I PREPARE

Specific procedures for Shelter-in-Place at an ambulatory site may include the following:

  • Quickly lock exterior doors and windows

  • Close the office

Fire

Back

A Code Red should be immediately called whenever any of the following indications of a real or suspected fire are experienced:

  • Seeing smoke or a fire
  • Smelling smoke or other burning material
  • Feeling unusual heat on a wall, door or other surface

YOUR RESPONSIBILITIES

  • Be completely familiar with the Fire Response Manual and your role
  • Participate in all fire drills and practice sessions as required
  • Attend all fire training classes when assigned
  • Learn the fire alarm code and how to report a fire
  • Learn the locations of and how to operate fire alarm boxes, and fire extinguishers
  • Know evacuation procedures and routes
  • Observe the "No Smoking" rules
  • Never store flammable liquids in your desk or cabinet
  • Report any defective wiring, such as frayed cords, loose or broken plugs, blown fuses, etc.
  • Properly dispose of waste or rags used with cleaning solvents
  • Do not use special heating units until all safety factors have been complied with (i.e., portable heaters, electric coffee makers)

SAFETY DO’S

  • Keep hallways clear of furniture, surplus, and equipment
  • Know the location of fire exits in your work area
  • Know evacuation routes for your work area and what to do in case of a fire
  • Smoke only in designated outdoor smoking areas
  • Keep your work area clean and free of clutter
  • Learn patient transportation and evacuation techniques

IN CASE OF FIRE Prior to opening any door during a fire, feel the door and the doorknob. If either is hot, do not open the door. If the door and knob are cool, stand to the side of the door and open the door slowly. If you must enter a fire scene, stay low.

Remember that smoke and heat rise to the ceiling and you must crawl beneath them. In the event of a fire in your area, you should assist in escorting all patients, visitors and staff to a safe area as needed.

Active Shooter

Back

RUN:

If it safe to do so, the first course of action should be to run away from the area of danger until you are in a safe location

Visualize possible escape routes, including physically accessible routes for patients, visitors, and staff

Avoid using elevators

Leave your personal belongings behind

Take others with you, but do not stay behind because others will not go

HIDE:

Hide or Shelter-in-place means to take immediate shelter whenever you are

It may also mean closing and locking your designated areas as quickly as possible and take steps to prevent others from coming in

Esure that patients, staff and visitors are secure in a room

Move out of the hallways and other open areas into the nearest office, conference room or patient room

Close and lock door.(use furniture and other items to block the entrance if the door does not have a lock

Do not leave the building

FIGHT:

If neither running nor hidding is a safe option, as a last resort when confronted by the shooter, adults in immediate danger should consider trying to disrupt or incapaciate the shooter by using aggressive force and items in the environment, such as: IV poles, chairs, fire extinguishers, etc

Extreme Weather

Back

Patient Care Areas

  • Close Shades or drapes over all windows
  • Remove all items not essential for patient care and place in drawers or lockers
  • Secure non-patient care areas by removing items and placing them in lockers
  • Advise and reassure patients that the above precautions are taken whenever there is a severe weather warning
  • Lower beds to lowest position. Ensure patients have their nurse call button
  • Provide all patients with a blanket or bedspread which may be used to protect them if necessary.
  • Move all charts, emergency carts, etc. to an inside room
  • Prepare equipment that may be needed to move patients in the event of a High Wind event including:
    • Blanets
    • Wheelchairs
    • Linens
    • Patient care equipment

Safety Guidelines in Non-Patient Care Areas

  • Close all blinds, shades and drapes.
  • Remove loose objects from desk and counter tops and window sills.
  • Secure all wheeled carts in your work area.

High Wind Events

  • Staff will begin to move patients to a safe area away from windows
  • Place blankets, linens and mattresses on the floor of safe areas to provide a place for patients to lie down.
  • Move patient care equipment needed to care for patients to the safe area.
  • Maintain appropriate space in corridors for personnel to move safety among patients.
  • Close all doors.
  • Staff will assist patients in lying flat or crouching down with head covered with blankets.

Emergency Response

  • Ensure that all patients are informed, safe and comfortable.
  • Check that all communications systems are functioning. If systems are down, initiate actions to restore service or use other resources (i.e., walkie-talkies, sat phones, messengers).
  • Review all disaster assessment reports; if necessary, the CEO will determine whether a partial or total evacuation of the hospital is necessary.(See Hospital Evacuation Plan)
  • All resulting casualties will be treated according to the Disaster Plan.
  • Provide status reports to the MAC via Reddi Net, Command Aware, H.E.A.R., or telephone.

Emergency Response

When the "All Clear" is given the following procedures shall be instituted

  • All staff will assist in restoring the environment to normal operations.
  • Staff will assess their department for damage and safety hazards, if there are no safety concerns, patients may be moved back to their rooms. If a patient’s room is not in habitable condition, alternate accommodations shall be made.
  • See also USC Emergency Response/ Business Continuity Plan
